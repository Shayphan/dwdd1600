Animation Guides

The Animation Workflow

Before you begin to make any type of animation it's important to understand what your workflow will be like from the beginning to ensure a smooth and efficient workflow. We can split it up into 3 major sections:

  1. Pre-Production Blueprinting
  2. Production: Building the Motion
  3. Post Production: Final Tweaks + Exporting

Pre-Production Blueprint Stage

Scripting and Storyboarding

Before you can turn your artwork into works of masterpieces you must start from the very beginning of scripting and storyboarding out all your ideas, characters, and story. It doesn't have to be super detailed, but jotting down your ideas in this stage gives you a clear direction of where to take your work when you head into production.
Scripting
Scripting is the act of writing down the dialogue and action of what characters will say and do in an animated work and describe the setting and actions occuring.
Storyboarding
Storyboarding is the visual version of scripting. Thumbnail sketches are used to visually show where characters will be set in the scene, camera angles for certain shots. It is essentially a roadmap that helps you determine the flow, pacing, and layout of the animation before production begins.

Production: Animating your work

This is the stage where the fun happens. This is the point in your workflow where you begin to illustrate and animate your drawings to be moving scenes by using keyframes and In-betweening.

Keyframing
Keyframes are the major start and end points in an animation. These poses serve as a reference point for you to do your in-betweening and make it look smoother and flows better.
In-betweening
Also known as tweening, when you tween your animation you are essentially "filling in" the gaps of your major poses (keyframes) and drawing in every movement that the object or character is performing in order to go from one pose to another.
Onion Skins
When it comes to animating you will come across this term called onion skin. No we do not mean actual onions, we're talking about the technique used in both traditional and digital animation that displays a transparent outline of your previous drawings all simultaneously. This acts as a guide allowing the animator to track movement and illustrate smoothly and consistently.

Exporting

Great! Now that you've finished your animation and have finished production, it is now time to do any final polishes and get ready to export your work! There are many ways to export an animation depending on what program/software you are using. Our courses will be using Adobe animate.

12 Animation Principles

Learning the 12 basic animation principles is absolutely crucial to creating captivating animations. You will need to practice and remember the following:

  1. Squash and Stretch
  2. Anticipation
  3. Staging
  4. Follow through and Overlapping
  5. Straight Ahead and Pose-to-Pose
  6. Slow in and Slow Out
  7. Arcs
  8. Secondary Action
  9. Timing
  10. Exaggeration
  11. Solid Drawing
  12. Appeal
